Background

If science is fundamentally about experimentation, about discovering better, more effective ways of doing things, then it follows that we should also be willing to experiment with how research itself is conducted. Rethinking the structures, incentives, publication systems, and funding models that underpin research institutions could unlock entirely new modes of thinking, innovation, and discovery. This line of inquiry is at the heart of the growing field of metascience.

Academia and industry are both essential — and both incomplete

The world's research enterprise has long been anchored by two dominant institutions. Universities produce the majority of basic science: curiosity-driven, peer-reviewed, and publicly funded. Industry — from large corporate R&D to VC-backed startups — drives applied research and translates discoveries into products. Together, these systems have delivered extraordinary advances and materially improved human life. Both are also structurally limited in ways that create significant blind spots.

Many of the most pressing challenges ahead — climate, biological engineering, resilient health systems, new energy paradigms — require fundamental breakthroughs, not incremental discoveries or technology improvements. This is bottlenecked by our ability to pursue ambitious, coordinated, and high-risk work over long time horizons.

Where the system falls short

Despite their strengths, both academia and industry on average tend to produce incremental progress over step-change advances, and siloed research, both within disciplines and across the discovery–application divide.

Why this happens

These outcomes are not accidental — they are the direct result of underlying structures:

  • 1)
    Short-term funding cycles. In academia, ~3 year project-based grants. In industry, quarterly targets and fundraising timelines compress time horizons, making long bets difficult to sustain.
  • 2)
    Misaligned incentives. In academia, success is measured in publications, citations, and grant renewal. In industry, success is tied to commercialisation and near-term ROI. In both cases, the system rewards predictability over ambition.
  • 3)
    Fragmentation of expertise. Basic scientists, engineers, and product managers operate in largely separate worlds, with limited mechanisms for deep, sustained collaboration.
  • 4)
    Intolerance for failure. Funding, evaluation, and career structures penalise negative or null results, discouraging the kind of risk-taking required for breakthrough discovery.

What this leads to

  • Underinvestment in high-risk, high-reward ideas. Research funded by institutions that tolerate early failure, reward long-term success, and grant researchers substantial freedom produces a markedly higher share of high-impact work. Compared with scientists operating under short review cycles and predefined deliverables, these researchers not only generate more breakthrough papers but shift their work toward more novel and exploratory lines of inquiry.
  • Suppression of negative results, which remain unpublished and unshared — leading to duplicative work and slower cumulative progress.
  • Peer review conservatism. Most grant processes depend on politics and committees. By their nature, committees lead to median results, so committee-mediated grants will rarely fund unsexy outlier work.
  • A coordination gap. Research that requires tightly integrated teams to build new tools, platforms, or datasets — especially those without immediate commercial value — is difficult to execute. As a result, scientists often don't even think in these bigger, more ambitious ways, and instead focus on problems that fit within their existing constraints.

The gap

What falls through

The system struggles most with work that is too applied for academia, too early or non-commercial for industry, and too complex and coordinated for either to tackle alone.

If left unaddressed, these structural gaps don't just slow progress — they define its ceiling. Entire categories of breakthrough become systematically unlikely, not because they are impossible, but because no existing institution is designed to pursue them.

This is where frontier research lives: building tools and datasets that accelerate entire fields, tackling engineering problems too large for a single lab and too public-good-oriented for a startup, and pursuing high-risk bets that require years of coordinated effort.

The vision

Build more novel and diverse structures for research institutions

Beyond traditional academia and industry, these new institutions would be characterised by:

  • Longer-term, stable funding horizons
  • Explicit tolerance for early and ongoing failure
  • Success metrics that go beyond publications or profits
  • Where appropriate (e.g. for technology development), designed to attract talent that allows basic researchers and technologists to work closely, hand-in-hand

Instead, success would be defined by:

Radical breakthroughs

Field-shaping discoveries that change what is possible

Essential technologies

Development of research-enabling tools, datasets, and platforms that accelerate entire fields

Academia

Strengths

  • Long tradition of basic, curiosity-driven research
  • Strong talent pipeline and training
  • Freedom from commercial pressure
  • Wide collaborative networks across research disciplines
  • Oriented toward public goods and open knowledge

Structural weaknesses

  • Fragmentation — PI autonomy limits coordination, especially between basic researchers and engineers
  • Short grant cycles (2–5 years) and career incentives misaligned with long problems
  • Publication incentives crowd out tool and infrastructure building
  • Journal system discourages sharing negative results, contributing to duplicative work
  • Poorly suited to engineering-heavy, execution-focused work
  • Committee-mediated grants rarely fund unsexy outlier work
  • Projects whose outputs are structured data or reusable code don't lend themselves to papers

Industry & Startups

Strengths

  • Fast-moving, cross-functional execution
  • Strong engineering and product talent
  • Commercially viable research gets resourced quickly
  • Clear accountability and milestone culture
  • Access to proprietary data and infrastructure

Structural weaknesses

  • VC timelines and return expectations are typically designed for software
  • Massive emphasis on scale, growth, and returns
  • Short-termism — months to 3-year runway constraints
  • Commercialisation bias: non-profitable research starved
  • Speculative or basic science rarely funded
  • Outputs are IP, not public goods
  • Market logic incompatible with public-good research

The landscape

Six alternative structures

The following structures represent distinct approaches to organising, funding, and governing research outside of conventional academia and industry. For a comprehensive breakdown and comparison, see Structures.

Industrial LabsCorporate-funded, research-first
Corporate-funded research labs with significant autonomy from business units. Historically among the most productive research environments ever created — Bell Labs alone produced the transistor, Unix, C, and information theory. The model is rare today, eroded by corporate short-termism, but remains a critical archetype for what sustained, well-resourced research freedom can produce. Examples: Bell Labs, Xerox PARC, DuPont Experimental Station, GE Research, IBM Research, Ink and Switch.
Government & Mission-Oriented LabsPublicly funded, national mandate
Publicly funded institutions with long-term mandates tied to national missions — defence, energy, health, agriculture. Medium-to-high coordination, decades-long time horizons, strategic applied research. Own and operate shared infrastructure (accelerators, supercomputers, containment facilities) that no individual institution could sustain. Examples: CSIRO, Battelle Memorial Institute, US DOE National Labs, Fraunhofer Society (Germany), RIKEN (Japan).
Core-funded Nonprofit InstitutesPhilanthropic, redesigned incentives
A newer generation of institutes that intentionally redesign incentives, funding structures, and shared infrastructure while retaining PI-like autonomy. Funded at ~8-year horizons, with strong shared platforms enabling more coordination than traditional institutes — but not yet fully integrated teams. Built on the premise that excellent scientists need better conditions, not different people. Examples: Arc Institute, CZI Biohub, Francis Crick Institute, Howard Hughes Medical Institute.
Focused Research OrganisationsMission-driven, time-bound, in-house
Standalone organisations built to execute a specific technical goal with a unified team. CEO-led, full-time cross-functional staff, milestone-driven — pursuing public goods rather than products. Time-bound (~3–7 years): once the mission is complete, the FRO shuts down. FRO-shaped problems need a clear goal from day one, tight coupling between workstreams, and strong conviction about the right approach. Examples: E11 Bio, Cultivarium, Parallel Squared Technology Institute (all via Convergent Research).
BBNs / Frontier Research ContractsContract-based, distributed elite teams
Contract-based networks assembling elite teams across institutions to tackle defined frontier problems. Named after the original Bolt Beranek and Newman lab, which used government contracts to build foundational internet infrastructure while pursuing bolder agendas on the margins. More financially precarious than FROs but more flexible. Ideal for engineering-heavy work too complex for academia and too public-good-oriented for industry. Examples: BBN Technologies, SRI International, MITRE Corporation.
ARPA-style OrganisationsHigh-risk, programme-managed, external
Agencies that fund and coordinate external research teams toward ambitious goals. Defined by empowered programme managers, milestone-driven portfolio management, and explicit tolerance for failure. Suited to problems where the right approach is unknown — ARPA programs run parallel tracks to test multiple approaches simultaneously, pruning and recombining without organisational disruption. Programmes typically run 3–5 years. Examples: DARPA, ARPA-E, ARPA-H, ARIA (UK), SPRIND (Germany), Speculative Technologies, Actuate, Wellcome Leap.
